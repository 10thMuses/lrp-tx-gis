[Owner],

Attached is the memo on drilling around Caramba North, written so it can go straight to the buyer. The short version: **the public record supports that the buyer's drilling-vibration concern is not borne out at this site.**

**The spine of the memo is location.** The activity that produces ground vibration is hydraulic fracturing of deep horizontal wells — and that activity is simply not occurring at or near the tract: **no drilling permit of any kind has been filed within ten miles since 2020**, **no well of any kind has been spudded within two miles in over a decade** (none since before 2015; no shallow well within two miles since 2002), and only ~2% of recent county drilling is within ten miles (the rest a median ~30 miles away). The only wells near the tract are **old (1950s–2002), plugged, shallow vertical legacy wells** — not active, not fracked, not a vibration source.

**Reinforcing that:** the drilling Pecos *does* see is disproportionately the low-intensity, non-fracked kind. Of wells actually drilled in Pecos since 2020, about half are shallow (under 3,000 ft), and — checked well-by-well against the Railroad Commission's own Wellbore Profile field — about **86% of those shallow wells are vertical** (conventional, unfracked). Pecos is far more weighted to shallow vertical drilling than comparable counties: roughly **50% of Pecos wells are shallow vs. ~2% across the peer counties**, whose programs are almost entirely deep horizontal. So even county-wide, Pecos sees comparatively little fracking — and essentially none of it near the site.

A few things worth your knowing:

- **This version is materially stronger than where I started, and it is defensible.** My first cut leaned only on proximity, because the headline RRC permit figure (~87% of *permit applications* horizontal) appeared to cut against a "shallow/vertical" argument. I went back and matched the wells **actually spudded** to the Commission's own orientation field: the shallow ones are ~86% **vertical**. The permits-vs-actually-drilled distinction is the key, and the memo states it plainly, so if the buyer's people raise the 87% permit number we have already addressed it with the agency's own well-level data rather than being rebutted by it.
- **The memo uses RRC's own Wellbore Profile field for the orientation claim** — not a depth assumption — so the reinforcing "shallow → vertical → not fracked" point is sourced to the regulator, which is the strongest possible footing.
- **The methods/limitations section is removed from the buyer copy, per your instruction.** For your awareness only: this is a drilling-record analysis, not a geotechnical vibration study. If the buyer wants engineering certainty, the clean next step is a short vibration assessment, which this data would underpin. I'd suggest we be ready to offer that rather than over-argue.
- A full Pecos-vs-peers comparison on the *orientation* field (rather than depth) would require additional data pulls for the peer counties; the depth-based peer comparison in the memo is sound and the Pecos shallow→vertical link is RRC-confirmed.

Net: a strong package built on location first — essentially no drilling, and no fracking-type activity, at or near the site — reinforced by the fact that Pecos drilling is disproportionately the low-intensity, non-fracked kind. Built to hold up if the buyer scrutinizes it. Happy to walk through it or adjust emphasis before it goes out.
